How Long Does It Take to Build a House in 2026?

Most new homes take 7 to 12 months from groundbreaking to move-in. Here's the full timeline broken down by phase, construction type, and what makes ICF homes different from wood frame.

The Short Answer

Most homes take 7 to 12 months to build in 2026, including pre-construction. Production homes can be done in 4 to 6 months. Custom homes often run 12 to 18 months. The biggest variables are permitting, crew experience, and how complex your design is.

7 mo Average construction time (build only)
12 mo Typical total timeline including pre-construction
4–6 mo Production/spec home fastest timeline
16+ mo Complex custom builds

Timeline by Construction Type

Not all homes are built the same way and the type of build you choose has the biggest impact on how long you'll wait to move in.

Build Type Timeline Notes
Production / Spec Home 4–6 months Pre-designed floor plans, bulk materials, efficient crew scheduling
Tract / Development Home 6–9 months Some customization options, builder manages the process
Custom Home (Contractor-Built) 10–16 months Fully custom design, more coordination required
Owner-Builder 12–24 months Acting as your own GC adds significant time and complexity
ICF Custom Home 10–14 months Similar to custom, requires experienced ICF crew for best results

Phase-by-Phase Breakdown

Every new home build follows roughly the same sequence of phases. Here's what each one takes and what can slow it down.

Phase 1: Design and Planning (4–12 weeks)

Before any dirt moves, you need a finished set of plans, a signed contract with your builder, and financing locked in. This phase is commonly underestimated. Architectural revisions, structural engineering, and lender timelines all happen here. Custom homes take longer than spec builds where plans are already done.

Phase 2: Permitting (2–12 weeks)

Permit timelines vary wildly depending on your municipality. Rural counties can turn permits in 2 to 4 weeks. High-growth markets like parts of Florida, Texas, and Tennessee can take 8 to 12 weeks or longer due to volume. You cannot break ground until permits are issued, so factor this in early.

Phase 3: Site Preparation (1–3 weeks)

Site clearing, grading, and excavation. Straightforward on flat, cleared lots. Can take longer on wooded or sloped sites, or if unexpected soil conditions require additional engineering.

Phase 4: Foundation (2–4 weeks)

For a standard concrete footing and slab, expect 2 to 4 weeks depending on size and curing time. ICF basement walls are poured during this phase and take similar time. Cold weather extends curing time significantly.

Phase 5: Wall Construction (2–6 weeks)

This is where ICF and wood frame diverge. An experienced ICF crew can stack and pour an entire home's above-grade walls in 1 to 3 weeks. Wood framing typically runs 2 to 6 weeks for a comparable structure. The catch: ICF requires a crew that has done it before. A new crew learning on the job will take as long or longer than wood frame.

ICF wall construction tip: Always ask your contractor how many ICF homes they have completed. An experienced crew treats wall stacking and concrete pours as a system, not an experiment. This is the single biggest factor in keeping the wall phase on schedule.

Phase 6: Rough-In Systems (4–8 weeks)

Electrical, plumbing, and HVAC rough-in happens after walls are up. This phase takes similar time on ICF and wood-frame builds. With ICF, HVAC systems are typically smaller because the envelope is so much tighter, which can simplify this phase.

Phase 7: Insulation and Drywall (2–4 weeks)

With ICF, insulation is already part of the wall system. You skip the spray foam or batt insulation step entirely. Drywall goes directly onto the ICF furring strips. This phase is typically faster on an ICF build than wood frame.

Phase 8: Interior and Exterior Finishes (6–12 weeks)

Flooring, cabinetry, trim, paint, fixtures, and exterior work all happen in this phase. This is where most of the remaining schedule variability lives. Material lead times, subcontractor availability, and the level of finish you've chosen all affect this timeline.

Phase 9: Inspections and Certificate of Occupancy (1–3 weeks)

Final inspections, punch list items, and the CO process round out the project. Most municipalities turn final inspections around in 1 to 2 weeks once you request them.

ICF vs. Wood Frame: Which Builds Faster?

The honest answer is that total project timelines are similar for a comparable custom home. But the differences in specific phases matter:

Phase Wood Frame ICF
Wall construction 2–6 weeks 1–3 weeks (experienced crew)
Insulation 1–2 weeks (separate step) Included in walls — no extra step
HVAC sizing Larger system required Smaller system, simpler install
Weather delays More vulnerable during framing Concrete pour weather-dependent but walls are solid once poured
Total timeline 10–14 months (custom) 10–14 months (custom)

Where ICF wins isn't necessarily speed — it's what you get at the end. A home that uses 50 to 70% less energy, withstands 200+ mph winds, and stands for generations. The timeline is similar, the result is categorically different.

What Makes Your Timeline Longer or Shorter

Factors that add time:

  • Permitting backlogs: High-growth counties can add 2 to 3 months before you break ground
  • Inexperienced crew: A contractor new to ICF or custom building will slow every phase down
  • Design changes mid-build: Any change order after framing starts ripples through every subsequent phase
  • Material lead times: Custom windows, specialty finishes, and appliances can add weeks if not ordered early
  • Weather: Concrete pours cannot happen in freezing temperatures or heavy rain
  • Financing delays: Draw schedules and lender inspections add time if not managed proactively

Factors that compress the timeline:

  • Experienced contractor: The biggest single variable. A contractor with 20 ICF builds under their belt runs a fundamentally tighter schedule
  • Simple rectangular floor plan: Fewer corners, no curves, standard ceiling heights — all reduce construction time
  • Pre-ordered materials: Locking in windows, doors, and finishes before walls are up eliminates waiting
  • Established subcontractor relationships: A GC with their own electrical, plumbing, and HVAC crews avoids scheduling gaps
  • Clear, complete plans: No ambiguity means no stops to figure things out on site

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to build an ICF house specifically?

An ICF custom home typically runs 10 to 14 months from groundbreaking to move-in, similar to a comparable wood-frame custom home. The wall phase can be faster with an experienced ICF crew, but overall timelines are similar. The advantage of ICF shows up in long-term performance, not necessarily construction speed.

Is ICF faster to build than wood frame?

For the wall phase, yes, an experienced ICF crew can stack and pour an entire home's walls in 1 to 3 weeks. Wood framing for a comparable home typically takes 2 to 6 weeks. However, ICF requires concrete cure time and experienced labor. Total project timelines are generally similar.

What causes the most delays in new home construction?

Permitting backlogs, inexperienced subcontractors, design changes mid-build, and material lead times are the most common culprits. In high-growth markets, permitting alone can add 2 to 3 months before groundbreaking. Choosing a contractor with established relationships and a track record of on-schedule projects is the best protection against delays.

How long does permitting take for a new home?

Permitting ranges from 2 to 12 weeks depending on your municipality. Rural counties tend to be faster. High-growth suburban markets in states like Florida, Texas, and Tennessee can take longer due to volume. Always build permitting time into your project schedule before groundbreaking.
